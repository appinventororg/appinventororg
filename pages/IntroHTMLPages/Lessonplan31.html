Lesson Plan 3.1 Texting

Printable Version

Objectives

  • Learn how to send and respond to SMS texts
  • Gain experience with the TextToSpeech component.
  • Learn the basics of lists and for-each loops
  • Learn the basics of dynamic and persistent data.

Outline

1. Demonstrate the texting component

2. Students build the “Thinking of you” app that texts a message to a list of their friends.

3. Introduce lists and have the students re-code their app with a list and foreach.

4. No Texting While Driving tutorial

5. Texting: Conceptualize Worksheet

6. Texting: Customizations

Preparation

Building apps which send and respond to texts is incredibly motivating. If your students have devices with service (texting) plans, they are good to go. However, if your students don’t have a service plan, they’ll have to do some setup to get things ready.

Specifically, for phones that don’t have a service plan, the students will need to create a Google Voice account, install Google Voice on the phone, and use it and WiFi for the texting. It is a pretty easy setup, and once its set things work fine. But make sure you try it yourself before class.

1. Demonstrate the Texting Component (15)

  • If your students phones don’t have service plan, demonstrate how to get a Google Voice account and install Google Voice on their phones.
  • Drag out a texting component and a button
  • Demonstrate that there are three blocks to send a text, setting the Texting.Message, the Texting.PhoneNumber, and then sending it (Texting.SendMessage). If someone in class is up for it, put their number in and text them with the test app.
  • Demonstrate receiving a text with the Texting.MessageReceived. One fun sample is just to respond to any incoming text with the same message.
  • If you didn’t show the Raffle app earlier in the semester or want to show it again and maybe run another raffle. The app is described here: http://www.appinventor.org/raffle

2. Students build a one-click texting app (no lists) (30)

Ask the students to build an app that allows them to text three friends a message with a single click. For this exercise, don’t provide a step-by-step tutorial, just let the students build it. Don’t introduce lists yet; the expectation of this first exercise is that they’ll copy and paste blocks to text the three friends.

3. Introduce Lists

In the preceding section, some students may have realized they could use a list for the exercise. Great, use their input to lead into an introduction of lists. You might even have them demo their solution. In any case, be sure and go over:

  • Defining a global variable to hold the phone numbers
  • Plugging in a make a list blocks and clicking the blue icon to give it three slots
  • Plugging in three text blocks for the three numbers.
  • The foreach block for looping through and texting all in the list
  • emphasize that if there are 3 items in the list, the blocks inside foreach will be executed 3 times.
  • emphasize that the item parameter is the current item being processed
  • trace the code on the whiteboard, showing how the parameter item changes.
  • Mention that this foreach solution, unlike a solution which copy-pastes blocks, will work even with dynamic data (e.g., a list of numbers that the user can input and change).

Assign the students the task of actually modifying their app to use a list and foreach. This will give them experience with the mechanics of it.

The learning nugget at: http://www.appinventor.org/Lists#textAll has a solution.

4. No Text While Driving Tutorial

As an in-class assignment or homework, assign students the No Text While Driving tutorial:

http://www.appinventor.org/notext-steps

The students will gain experience with responding to texts (The Texting.MessageReceived block), the TextToSpeech component, and they’ll be introduced to persistence and TinyDB. Persistence is a difficult concept. The tutorial will introduce it and you’ll need to provide additional conceptual lessons to fortify the student’s understanding.

5. Texting: Conceptual Worksheet(45)

Hand out the document below and ask the students to form groups of two or three to discuss. After a few minutes, lead a full-class discussion of the first few questions, then repeat the process.

Conceptualize and Customize Worksheet:

https://docs.google.com/document/d/1R9eoiyxnCZtNV3eisxkJGuG-l7EVB-p7oys-qBA_SzA/edit#heading=h.ubd6xcgo6ng

The worksheet presents some difficult terminology. Here are some simple ways to define the terms:

persistent data -- data that lives on even after an app is closed and re-opened.

dynamic data-- data that is not fixed, but is created/edited as an app runs, often through end-user input (can also be data from the web, etc.).

Here is a learning nugget on persistence: http://www.appinventor.org/Persistence

6. Customization Exercises (30-90)

Assign the students the customization exercises in: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1R9eoiyxnCZtNV3eisxkJGuG-l7EVB-p7oys-qBA_SzA/edit#heading=h.ubd6xcgo6ng

The first three questions ask the students to code more complex text auto-responsing, e.g., sending a special text to a particular number.

The fourth question is more difficult and concerns persistent data. It requires the students to create input forms for entering the phone numbers of the dynamic list (and the custom message), and to follow the pattern for storing and retrieving this data from the TinyDB. Probably this should only be assigned to advanced students, and this topic will be covered later in the course.
