I Have a Dream:
Conceptualize, Customize, Create

Printable Version

Objectives

  • Understand terminology and the basics of an app’s architecture
    • components and properties
    • event handlers
    • conditionals
  • Encourage creativity and exploration

Outline

  1. Small group discussion of concepts/terminology
  2. Customization exercises
  3. Creative project

Preparation

Make copies of the “I Have a Dream” Conceptualize and Customize Worksheet

1. Small group discussion of concepts/terminology (45)

Hand out the above document and ask the students to form groups of two or three to discuss. After a few minutes, lead a full-class discussion of the first few questions, then repeat the process.

Emphasize terminology-- coding is a complete foreign language to most students. Draw diagrams of an app’s architecture, e.g., something like the picture in chapter 14 of the App Inventor book: http://appinventor.org/Architecture2

You can assign students the homework task of writing out answers to the discussion questions, and/or emphasize that the questions are similar to what will appear on a quiz.

2. Customization Exercise (45)

The first exercise is about having a speech begin when the phone is shaken. Its purpose is to generalize the idea of event handler-- in the tutorial a button click triggers the action, but it can just as easily be triggered by a different event.

The second exercise concerns using only a single Player component and switching its Source property dynamically to the file you want to play. The purpose is to show that component properties can be changed at run-time (in the blocks). Make it clear that the Player component is really set up like a music player for which you can change the station by changing the Source.

3. Creative Project

At this stage, the students understand some basics about event handling and conditionals, enough to build some simple “Media Board” apps with simple “click” behaviors that show or play media. A sample project page is provided at appinventor.org/soundboard

The students will come up with many ideas and you will need to both encourage their exploration and choosing the proper scope/complexity based on the current limitations of their programming knowledge.

  • Tell the students to work in groups of 2. The collaboration spurs creativity but keep groups small, or have people work as individuals, so everyone learns the coding. Pairing students of similar experience/skill level seems to work so as to avoid one student (the more skilled) doing the work.
  • Show/Discuss some sample projects. There are some listed at at appinventor.org/soundboard. Encourage them to build something that someone will actually use, whether it be an app for a class they are taking, one for their little sister, or one they might use themselves.
  • Emphasize that this is a project for “publication”, so they should allocate time for refining and finishing. This means refining the user interface, making sure the app has an icon and good title, and testing that the user interface is easily understandable.
  • Go over the publication instructions at appinventor.org/appPage. Discuss the options for publishing which include 1) on their portfolio, 2) on the App Inventor gallery, and 3) on Google Play Store, and 4) emailing the app to their friends and family
